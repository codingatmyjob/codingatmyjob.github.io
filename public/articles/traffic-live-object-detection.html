Overview

I recently decided I was going to give myself 10 days to study for and pass AI-900 before it gets retired June 30, 2026. I reached the computer vision section of study and thought it would be fun to try and build something lightweight in the browser. There are hundreds of different methods for models and object detection, but I wanted to make something that I could display here.

Going into this I had absolutely zero experience with object detection but I had 2 main goals:

  1. Use a live traffic feed as my data source (don't care where from)
  2. Run entirely client-side

Live Feed

Below is a TensorFlow.js object detection demonstration that runs fully in the browser. The HLS.js library enables a live HTTPS stream sourced from the Iowa Department of Transportation. That stream is then fed into the TensorFlow.js COCO-SSD (Common Objects in Context Single Shot MultiBox Detection) model, which runs lightweight object detection directly in the browser.

Challenges

Unlike past projects I think I did a better job of staying within the original scope! A lot of research went into actually finding an open feed that didn't require an API or some kind of proxy service to get around CORS. On top of that, these lightweight object detection models are very input sensitive (they require a high quality video, at a similar angle to what they were trained on).

This meant even though I had multiple feeds that fit my criteria, I also need a camera angle that enabled the model to work properly. I ended up finding a feed from the Iowa DOT that sits directly over an intersection with a red light, pointed straight down onto the oncoming traffic below. This ended up being the best camera I could find that met the criteria.

I found myself compromising on the model choice as well. I had 3 options available:

  • lite_mobilenet_v2 - fastest but least accurate model
  • mobilenet_v1 - middle ground model where we sacrifice some accuracy for a noticeable boost in speed
  • mobilenet_v2 - the most accurate but also the slowest model

Even though mobilenet_v1 is the oldest of the models, it ended up being the best model for our use case. I saw a noticeable boost in accuracy over the lite version, and the mobilenet_v2 model was just too slow to be usable in a live feed scenario without a dedicated processor.

I also had to include an inference fallback based on device hardware; WebGL -> WASM -> CPU. WebGL is picked with the highest priority when a device has a GPU/hardware acceleration enabled, WASM is the fallback for browsers where WebGL is unavailable, and CPU last so the demo still runs even in constrained environments.

Finally, I had difficulty accounting for fullscreen. If a user tried to use the native fullscreen button, the object detection didn't follow. I had to make sure the fullscreen functionality applied to the video and canvas elements, and not just the video like the native button did.

Final Thoughts

With a heavier model, or an actual backend to process inference/video input, I could get this working with much higher speed and accuracy. But, constraints are constraints and I didn't want scope creep to take over the project.
